\begin{abstract}
Multi-speaker meeting transcription is fragile because overlapping speech, interruptions, ambiguous speaker boundaries, low-energy speech, and domain-specific terms jointly degrade automatic speech recognition (ASR) and downstream correction. The bottleneck is not only recognizing words, but knowing which parts of a fluent transcript are supported by audio, speaker-time evidence, retrieval evidence, or correction audits. We propose \tw, an evidence-grounded conversation-map framework that treats chaotic meeting transcription as an auditing problem rather than merely an ASR or summarization problem. \tw combines ASR hypotheses, diarization turns, temporal anchors, overlap indicators, retrieved domain terms, constrained correction proposals, and audit flags into a traceable representation for review. Across existing public-data subsets and controlled safety studies in this repository, meeting speech is substantially harder than read speech, the automatic map runs on English and Mandarin meeting clips, conservative RAG improves base-model Earnings-22 term recall from 0.833 to 1.000 without changing WER on the frozen v3 subset, and leakage audits show that learned correction gates remain unsafe without stronger held-out evidence.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern speech systems can produce fluent transcripts from a single speaker, and large language models (LLMs) can make those transcripts look cleaner. Meetings are a different setting. Speakers interrupt each other, speak over each other, leave partial turns unfinished, use low-energy backchannels, and mention technical or financial terms that are rare in ASR training data. A clean paragraph can therefore hide exactly the information a meeting record should preserve: who spoke, when it happened, whether speech overlapped, which term was retrieved, and whether a correction was supported.

This paper uses the following central framing: chaotic meeting transcription should be treated as an evidence-grounded auditing problem, not merely an ASR or summarization problem. The objective is not to claim state-of-the-art transcription. The objective is to make uncertainty inspectable before a user trusts a corrected meeting record.

Two challenges motivate the design. First, read-speech ASR performance can overstate robustness on meetings. In the repository's fixed public-data subsets, a base model obtains Mandarin FLEURS CER 0.113, while AISHELL-4 meeting subsets remain much harder: CER 0.610 on the 12-clip formal subset and CER 0.537 on the 60-clip subset \cite{TODO_fleurs,TODO_aishell4}. English AMI meetings also remain difficult, with base WER 0.398 in the formal subset and 0.349 in a 24-clip held-out subset \cite{TODO_ami}. Second, post-ASR correction can introduce unsupported edits. Retrieval can rescue rare terms, but irrelevant candidates can bias a correction module; learned gating can also look strong when it sees leakage-prone audit fields.

\tw addresses these challenges by converting speech-system artifacts into a temporal-anchor conversation map. Each map item preserves raw ASR text, timestamps, speaker labels when available, overlap flags, retrieved terms, proposed corrections, and audit outcomes. The Streamlit product and detective-style UI are therefore research artifacts: they expose evidence and uncertainty for inspection rather than serving as the main algorithmic claim.

The contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item We formulate chaotic meeting transcription as an evidence-auditing task over ASR, diarization, overlap, retrieval, and correction provenance.
\item We implement a temporal-anchor conversation map that preserves raw text and traceable evidence for each proposed modification.
\item We evaluate the pipeline on existing public subsets of FLEURS, AMI, AISHELL-4, and Earnings-22, while explicitly separating real-public, controlled, proxy, and mock/demo evidence.
\item We report both positive and negative results: conservative RAG can improve domain-term recall without transcript drift on a small blind subset, while EvidenceGate leakage and independent held-out failures show the limits of automated correction decisions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Multi-speaker ASR and diarization.}
Multi-speaker meeting processing must connect lexical content to speaker identity and time. DiarizationLM shows that a compact textual representation of ASR and diarization output can be post-processed with LLMs to improve speaker-attributed transcripts \cite{wang2024diarizationlm}. DM-ASR uses diarization as an explicit structural prior for speaker- and time-conditioned ASR queries \cite{li2026dmasr}. TagSpeech proposes end-to-end multi-speaker ASR and diarization with fine-grained temporal grounding \cite{huo2026tagspeech}. \tw differs in scope: it does not train a new speech model or optimize an end-to-end ASR objective. It adapts the speaker-time representation idea into an auditable post-processing layer over existing ASR and diarization outputs.

\paragraph{Overlap-aware meeting processing.}
Overlap and interruptions are central failure modes in real meetings. A diarization system can give speaker turns and overlap estimates, but overlap F1 can remain low even when DER is usable. In the repository's AMI held-out diarization result, pyannote obtains DER 0.106 and overlap F1 0.490; on the AISHELL-4 60x20 subset, DER is 0.327 and overlap F1 is 0.262. \tw uses overlap as a risk signal for correction and review, not as a claim that overlap is solved.

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented ASR correction.}
Retrieval-augmented correction has been proposed for rare named-entity ASR errors by retrieving candidate entities and passing them to a correction model \cite{pusateri2024retrieval}. \tw uses a smaller and more conservative variant: local glossary retrieval supplies candidate domain terms, but a correction can be rejected or routed to review if evidence is weak. This design is motivated by the repository's RAG v2 negative result, where RAG helped the weaker tiny ASR model but reduced term F1 for the stronger base model.

\paragraph{LLM post-processing and correction safety.}
LLMs can make ASR text fluent, but fluency is not evidence. \tw therefore keeps raw transcripts, candidate terms, correction proposals, and gate decisions side by side. The controlled safety experiments and EvidenceGate audit are treated as safety analyses rather than as real-world correction performance.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given an audio recording $x$, an ASR system emits a sequence of transcript segments
$A=\{a_i\}$, where each segment includes text and optional word timestamps. A diarization system emits speaker-time intervals $D=\{d_j\}$. Retrieval over a domain glossary returns candidate terms $R_i$ for segment $a_i$. A correction module may propose corrected text $c_i$. \tw constructs a conversation map
$M=\{m_i\}$ in which each item records:
\[
m_i = (t_i^{start}, t_i^{end}, s_i, y_i, o_i, R_i, c_i, q_i),
\]
where $s_i$ is the attributed speaker or unknown speaker, $y_i$ is raw ASR text, $o_i$ is an overlap indicator, and $q_i$ is an audit state such as accepted, rejected, or needs review. \tw does not train a speech model and does not introduce a task loss; it organizes model artifacts and correction decisions into a reviewable representation.

\subsection{Overall Architecture}

Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} summarizes the pipeline. Audio is normalized and passed to ASR. Diarization produces speaker-time evidence. Alignment connects ASR segments or words to speaker turns. Overlap and interruption candidates are derived from temporal speaker evidence. A RAG module retrieves small candidate term lists from a local knowledge base. Correction modules operate under constraints: timestamps and speaker labels are preserved, unsupported changes are audited, and uncertain overlap regions can be routed to review.

\subsection{Temporal-Anchor Conversation Map}

The conversation map is the central data object. Each item stores start and end time, speaker label, raw text, corrected text if any, overlap status, confidence or review status, retrieved terms, and correction provenance. This follows the temporal-grounding motivation of recent multi-speaker ASR work, but it remains a post-processing format rather than an end-to-end model. In the repository, this behavior is implemented around the alignment, overlap, reference evidence, workflow, and export modules, and it is summarized by the automatic workflow CSV files listed in the claim audit.

\subsection{Speaker-Time and Overlap Evidence Extraction}

Speaker evidence comes from pyannote diarization when the environment is configured; deterministic mock paths remain available for demos but are not used as performance claims. Alignment assigns transcript material to speaker-time intervals and marks overlap when multiple speaker intervals cover a region. Interruption candidates are generated from temporal relationships and were manually reviewed only at the event level in this project; this yields candidate precision evidence but not recall or F1.

\subsection{Conservative RAG-Based Domain-Term Recovery}

The RAG module retrieves domain terms from local Markdown and glossary files. Unlike a generic meeting chatbot, it is used only as an auxiliary ASR term-recovery mechanism. The v3 policy is conservative: retrieved candidates can support substitutions for domain terms, but weak evidence is rejected or routed to review. The Earnings-22 experiments evaluate this mechanism on finance-call audio, which supports the term-recovery contribution but not the overlap or diarization contribution.

\subsection{Correction Audit and Evidence Provenance}

Every correction proposal keeps raw text, proposed text, candidate terms, overlap status, and decision metadata. Controlled overlap-safety and binary safe-to-apply experiments evaluate whether evidence-aware correction decisions reduce unsafe edits in authored stress tests. EvidenceGate adds a learned decision model over audit features, but its leakage audit and independent held-out failure make it a limitation rather than a deployable main result.

\subsection{RQ3: Can Conservative RAG Improve Term Recall Without Degrading Quality?}

RQ3 uses Earnings-22 because finance calls contain domain terms and are already present as public-audio term-recovery artifacts. Table~\ref{tab:rag} shows both the positive v3 result and the earlier v2 negative result. On the frozen 6-file v3 subset, base ASR term recall improves from 0.833 to 1.000 with unchanged WER 0.212 for both the evidence-gate and LLM-verifier RAG variants. The term F1 remains 0.833, so the correct claim is term-recall recovery without WER drift, not overall transcript-quality improvement.

The v2 blind subset explains why the method is conservative. On 12 files, RAG improves tiny term F1 from 0.889 to 0.931, but reduces base term F1 from 0.972 to 0.931 and slightly worsens WER. This is a negative result against unrestricted correction and motivates rejecting weak evidence.

\subsection{RQ4: What Do Safety and Leakage Analyses Reveal?}

RQ4 asks whether automated correction decisions can be trusted. The answer is mixed and is intentionally reported with negative results. Controlled overlap fixtures show that overlap-aware correction can avoid forbidden changes in authored high-overlap cases: overlap-aware rule and LLM variants reach safety pass rate 1.0 on high-overlap groups, while a no-overlap-awareness rule variant has safety pass rate 0.0 and three forbidden changes in true high-overlap cases. This supports the design principle that overlap should route uncertain edits to audit, but it is not a real-audio correction benchmark.

EvidenceGate is the clearest leakage lesson. An audit-aware grouped test reaches macro F1 1.000, but the repository documents that this uses label-proxy and final-audit fields. A feature audit categorizes 41 fields: 19 allowed pre-decision, 4 risky reference-derived, 14 direct label proxy, and 4 final audit outcome fields. After stricter feature filtering, the evidence-only random forest still reaches grouped-test macro F1 0.976, but on the independent held-out set the best strict model reaches only macro F1 0.325, with needs-review recall 0.033 and unsafe-accept rate 0.167. Table~\ref{tab:safety} summarizes these results, and Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:app_evidence_leakage}--\ref{fig:app_binary_f1} provide secondary charts.

\subsection{Secondary Track: Multilingual and Local Deployment Trade-offs}

The project also includes two required secondary tracks: multilingual evaluation and local deployment trade-off analysis. The multilingual evidence is not a separate claim of cross-lingual robustness; it is used to prevent the method from being described as English-only. The formal ASR summary covers English, French, and Mandarin read speech in FLEURS, plus English AMI and Mandarin AISHELL-4 meeting subsets. In the formal read-speech subset, the base model obtains FLEURS English WER 0.114, French WER 0.227, and Mandarin CER 0.113. In contrast, Mandarin meeting speech remains much harder: AISHELL-4 60x20 base CER is 0.537 and small CER is 0.482. The automatic conversation-map workflow also runs on Mandarin AISHELL-4 scored clips, but this remains a small-subset result rather than a full multilingual benchmark.

The local deployment track is similarly bounded. Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:app_rtf} and Table~\ref{tab:app_mobile} report local-machine whisper.cpp Level 1 rows. On AMI, whisper.cpp base has WER 0.351, cleaned WER 0.280, and RTF 0.056; tiny is faster with RTF 0.031 but less accurate, with WER 0.382 and cleaned WER 0.314. These rows support a speed/accuracy trade-off discussion for local inference, not a phone-device deployment claim. The repository also contains mobile-style proxy artifacts, but the paper treats true mobile deployment as future work.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

\paragraph{Meeting difficulty is supported, but not full benchmark performance.}
The public-data subsets consistently show that meeting speech is harder than read speech. However, the reported results are small subsets: 50 clips in the formal manifest, 24 AMI held-out clips, 60 AISHELL-4 clips, and small Earnings-22 blind subsets. The paper should not claim full-corpus performance or statistical significance.

\paragraph{The conservative workflow is a finding, not a weakness to hide.}
The safest current real workflow often preserves raw text, adds evidence, and routes risky regions to review rather than changing the transcript. This is consistent with the paper's framing: evidence-grounded auditing is useful even when correction is not automatically applied. The RAG v3 result is strongest when stated narrowly: base-model term recall improved from 0.833 to 1.000 on the 6-file Earnings-22 v3 subset with WER unchanged at 0.212.

\paragraph{Correction gates are not solved.}
EvidenceGate demonstrates the risk of leakage. Internal grouped-test results are high, but independent held-out performance is poor, especially for needs-review. Therefore, learned gating should be framed as a negative result and future-work path, not as a deployed component with proven generalization.

\paragraph{Interruption and overlap evidence remain limited.}
Ten reviewed interruption candidates all contained interruption behavior, but this is candidate precision only. There is no exhaustive timeline annotation, no sampled negative set, and no recall/F1. Overlap-aware safety is supported mainly by controlled fixtures; real overlap correction remains open.

\paragraph{Mobile deployment is not complete.}
The repository includes local-machine and proxy whisper.cpp results, but not a true phone-device deployment. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:app_mobile} records this boundary.

\paragraph{UI role.}
The detective-style Streamlit interface is important for inspection and presentation, but it is not the core algorithmic claim. Its role in the paper is to show how evidence maps, audit flags, and retrieved terms can be reviewed.

\section{Conclusion}

\tw treats chaotic meeting transcription as an evidence-grounded auditing problem. It builds temporal-anchor conversation maps that preserve ASR text, speaker-time evidence, overlap indicators, retrieved terms, and correction audits. Existing repository evidence supports a focused claim: meeting speech is harder than read speech on the tested public subsets, the automatic map runs on English and Mandarin meeting clips, and conservative RAG can recover domain-term recall on a small blind finance subset without changing WER. The most important limitations are also part of the result: correction gates are leakage-prone, independent held-out generalization is weak, interruption evaluation lacks recall, and true mobile-device deployment remains future work.
